What to do without the Puck

When do we forecheck

We forecheck when we are in pressure mode (out of possession)

When the other team gains control of the puck in their own end. We want to move into our forecheck.

Why do we forecheck?

Establishing Pressure and Forcing Turnovers

The primary goal of a successful forecheck is to disrupt the opponent's breakout options, limit their time and space, and regain puck possession deep in the offensive zone.

HOW WE DO IT?

  • Angling: Forcing the puck carrier toward the boards and cutting off ice width.
  • Stick Position: Keeping sticks on the ice to take away passing lanes.

1-2-2 Forecheck

We will use this system to apply pressure on the opposing team when they gain possession in their own end.

This system has one player positioned deep to apply immediate pressure on the puck carrier, while the other two players create a trapping formation to limit the opponent's options.

  • F1 (First Forward): Targets the puck carrier directly with aggressive pressure to force a play.
  • F2 (Second Forward):Stays with winger along boards to take away option. Ties up the opposing player.
  • F3 (Third Forward): Stays with the other winger in the middle to take away passing lanes.
  • Strongside D holds the line at the boards to prevent breakout attempts.
  • Weakside D stays inside middle but backs out early to be the last man back.

Forecheck Transitions and Scoring

Converting turnovers into scoring opportunities

If we take control of the puck after a successful forecheck, quickly transition to an offensive play. These are high percentage scoring chances.

  • Low-to-High Movement: Pulling won pucks out of corners back up to the point to shift the opposing coverage zone.
  • Cycle Attacks: Protecting the puck along the boards to wear down opposing defenders.
  • Crash Phase: Driving bodies directly to the netfront lane for screen targets and rebounds.
